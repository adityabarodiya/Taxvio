Income from Salary – Meaning, Taxability, Calculation, Deductions & Examples (FY 2025-26)

Quick Summary

Income from salary is one of the five heads of income under the Income Tax Act, 1961. If you work for an employer and receive a regular pay cheque, almost everything you earn — including allowances, bonuses, and certain employer-provided benefits — falls under this head. Understanding how your salary income is calculated, what is exempt, and how to reduce your tax liability legally can save you thousands of rupees every year. This guide covers everything from the basic definition to a step-by-step salary tax calculation for FY 2025-26.

What is Income from Salary?

In simple terms, income from salary is the total remuneration you receive from your employer against the services you provide. Under the Income Tax Act, 1961, "Salaries" is one of five heads of income — the others being house property, business or profession, capital gains, and other sources.

What makes salary income unique is that it is only taxable when there is a clear employer-employee relationship. Payments received for independent services or contracts do not fall under this head.

Meaning under Income Tax Act

The Income Tax Act does not give a single narrow definition of salary. Instead, it is broadly defined under Section 17(1) to include:

  • Wages
  • Annuity or pension
  • Gratuity
  • Fees, commissions, perquisites, and profits in lieu of salary
  • Advance salary
  • Leave encashment during service
  • Contributions made by the employer to a recognised provident fund in excess of the prescribed limit
  • Interest credited to a recognised provident fund in excess of the notified rate

This definition is deliberately wide so that no form of employment compensation escapes tax.

When is Salary Income Taxable?

Employer-Employee Relationship

Salary is taxable only when two conditions are met. First, there must be an employer-employee relationship — meaning the payer has control over how, when, and where the work is done. Second, payment must be received as consideration for services rendered.

⚠ Important: Salary from a former employer (e.g., arrears) and salary paid in advance are both taxable in the year they are received, not the year they relate to — unless relief under Section 89 is claimed.

Section 15 of Income Tax Act

Section 15 lays down when salary income is charged to tax. Salary is taxable on a due basis (when it becomes due) or a receipt basis (when actually received), whichever is earlier. This means even if your employer has not paid your January salary, it is taxable in January because it became due in that month.

Section 17 – Salary Definition

Section 17 breaks down the definition of salary into three sub-sections:

Sub-sectionWhat It Covers
Section 17(1)Core salary — wages, pension, gratuity, leave encashment, advance salary, annuity, fees, commission
Section 17(2)Perquisites — non-cash benefits provided by employer such as accommodation, car, club membership
Section 17(3)Profits in lieu of salary — compensation for termination, payment from unrecognised provident fund, keyman insurance policy

Components of Salary – Explained

A salary structure in India typically contains several components. Each component has its own tax treatment. Here is what you need to know:

1. Basic Salary

The fixed core pay before any allowances or benefits. It is 100% taxable. Basic salary forms the base for calculating HRA, PF contributions, and gratuity. Typically 40–50% of CTC.

2. Dearness Allowance (DA)

DA is paid to compensate employees for the rising cost of living. It is fully taxable. DA forms part of salary for HRA, gratuity, and PF calculation purposes.

3. House Rent Allowance (HRA)

HRA is provided to help employees meet rental expenses. It is partially exempt under Section 10(13A) — only for those living in rented accommodation. If you live in your own house, HRA is fully taxable. See our HRA Exemption Guide →

4. Leave Travel Allowance (LTA)

LTA covers travel costs for you and your family within India. It is exempt twice in a block of 4 years (current block: 2022–2025) subject to actual travel. International travel is not covered. Available only under the old tax regime.

5. Bonus & Commission

Performance bonuses, annual bonuses, and sales commissions are fully taxable as salary income in the year of receipt.

6. Special Allowances

These include city compensatory allowance, transport allowance, children education allowance (partially exempt at ₹100/month per child, max 2 children), and others. Most special allowances are taxable unless specifically exempt under Section 10(14).

7. Perquisites

Perquisites are non-cash benefits — such as a company car, rent-free accommodation, or interest-free loans. They are valued as per rules in Rule 3 of the Income Tax Rules and added to your taxable salary.

8. Pension

Commuted pension (lump sum) received by government employees is fully exempt. For private sector employees, one-third is exempt. Uncommuted pension (regular monthly pension) is fully taxable.

9. Advance Salary

Taxable in the year of receipt. Section 89 relief can be claimed if advance salary pushes you into a higher bracket in the year of receipt.

10. Arrears of Salary

Arrears received in the current year but relating to past years are taxable in the year of receipt. Claim Section 89 relief to spread the tax burden across the relevant years.

11. Gratuity

Gratuity at retirement is exempt up to ₹20 lakh for employees covered under the Payment of Gratuity Act. For others, exemption is computed under a formula. For government employees, it is fully exempt.

12. Leave Encashment

Leave encashment during service is fully taxable. At retirement, it is exempt up to ₹25 lakh for non-government employees under Section 10(10AA).

13. Provident Fund (PF)

Employee contribution to EPF is eligible for deduction under Section 80C. Employer's PF contribution exceeding 12% of salary (basic + DA) is taxable. Interest credited above 8.5% or on employee contributions exceeding ₹2.5 lakh per year is taxable from FY 2021-22 onwards.

14. ESOPs (Employee Stock Option Plans)

ESOPs are taxable at two stages. First, as a perquisite when you exercise the option (difference between fair market value and exercise price). Second, as capital gains when you sell the shares.

Deductions under Section 16 – Reducing Your Taxable Salary

After computing gross salary, the Income Tax Act allows certain deductions under Section 16 before arriving at your net taxable salary. These deductions are straightforward and available to all salaried individuals.

1. Standard Deduction — Section 16(ia)

The standard deduction is a flat deduction available to all salaried employees without the need for any proof or documentation. For FY 2025-26:

New Tax Regime

₹75,000

Increased from ₹50,000 in Budget 2024

Old Tax Regime

₹50,000

Unchanged

2. Professional Tax — Section 16(iii)

Professional tax is a state-level tax deducted by your employer and deposited with the state government. The maximum professional tax is ₹2,500 per year. The entire amount deducted is allowed as a deduction from your gross salary.

3. Entertainment Allowance — Section 16(ii)

This deduction is available only to government employees. The deduction is the least of:

  • ₹5,000
  • 20% of basic salary
  • Actual entertainment allowance received

Private sector employees do not get this deduction.

Gross Salary vs Taxable Salary — What's the Difference?

People often confuse these two terms. Here is the key distinction:

CTC vs Gross Salary vs In-Hand Salary

These three terms are frequently misunderstood — especially by those starting their first job. Here is a clear breakdown:

💡 Tip: When evaluating a job offer, always ask for a detailed salary breakup. A high CTC can be misleading if a large portion is gratuity or variable pay.

💡 Which regime should you choose?

Under the new regime, income up to ₹12 lakh is fully tax-free for salaried individuals (rebate under Section 87A + ₹75,000 standard deduction). If your total deductions — HRA + 80C + 80D + home loan + NPS — exceed approximately ₹3.75 lakh, the old regime may still save more tax. Tax Saving Tips for Salaried Employees (Old Regime)

If you opt for the old tax regime, here are the best legal ways to reduce your salary tax liability in FY 2025-26:

1. Maximise HRA Exemption

If you live on rent, ensure your employer correctly structures your HRA. Submit rent receipts for rent above ₹1 lakh annually (landlord's PAN needed). If your employer does not give HRA, you can still claim deduction under Section 80GG.

HRA Exemption Guide →

2. Invest Under Section 80C (Up to ₹1.5 Lakh)

This is the most widely used deduction. Eligible investments include EPF, PPF, ELSS mutual funds, NSC, 5-year FD, life insurance premiums, tuition fees for children, and home loan principal repayment.

Section 80C Investments →

3. NPS — Additional ₹50,000 under Section 80CCD(1B)

Invest in the National Pension Scheme (NPS) and claim an additional deduction of ₹50,000 per year over and above the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit. This is one of the most underutilised deductions.

4. Medical Insurance — Section 80D

Health insurance premium paid for yourself, spouse, children: up to ₹25,000. For parents below 60: additional ₹25,000. For senior citizen parents: up to ₹50,000. Total maximum deduction: ₹1 lakh.

5. Home Loan Interest — Section 24(b)

Interest on home loan for a self-occupied property is deductible up to ₹2 lakh per year. For let-out property, actual interest is deductible subject to overall loss set-off rules.

6. Leave Travel Allowance (LTA)

Claim LTA for domestic travel within a 4-year block. Only economy class airfare or AC-1 rail fare qualifies. Claim it for 2 trips in one block. Always keep travel tickets and boarding passes.

Common Mistakes While Filing Salary Income in ITR

✗

Not reporting income from multiple employers

Fix: If you changed jobs, combine Form 16 from both employers. Report total salary — not just the current employer's.

✗

Skipping interest income on savings accounts

Fix: Interest from savings accounts is taxable beyond ₹10,000 (or ₹50,000 for senior citizens) under Section 80TTA. Report it under 'Other Sources'.

✗

Claiming HRA without paying rent

Fix: Falsely claiming HRA exemption is a tax offence. Always have valid rent receipts. For rent above ₹1 lakh, landlord's PAN is mandatory.

✗

Not checking Form 26AS before filing

Fix: TDS deducted by your employer should match Form 26AS and AIS. Any mismatch can trigger a notice.

✗

Filing wrong ITR form

Fix: Use ITR-1 for income up to ₹50 lakh. Use ITR-2 if you have capital gains, foreign income, or income above ₹50 lakh.

✗

Missing the deadline without filing

Fix: The due date for salaried ITR is 31 July. Late filing attracts a fee under Section 234F of ₹1,000 (income up to ₹5L) or ₹5,000 (above ₹5L).

✗

Not claiming Section 89 relief on arrears

Fix: If you received salary arrears in FY 2025-26 relating to past years, file Form 10E before your ITR to claim Section 89 relief.

Frequently Asked Questions — Income from Salary

Q1. What is income from salary under the Income Tax Act?

Income from salary refers to any remuneration received by an individual from their employer in exchange for services rendered. It includes basic salary, allowances, perquisites, bonuses, commissions, and retirement benefits as defined under Section 17 of the Income Tax Act, 1961.

Q2. Is basic salary fully taxable?

Yes, basic salary is fully taxable. It forms the foundation of your salary structure and is added to your gross total income without any exemption.

Q3. What is the standard deduction for salaried employees in FY 2025-26?

For FY 2025-26, the standard deduction under Section 16(ia) is ₹75,000 for salaried individuals under the new tax regime and ₹50,000 under the old tax regime.

Q4. Which ITR form should salaried employees use?

Salaried employees should file ITR-1 (Sahaj) if their total income is up to ₹50 lakh and income is from salary, one house property, and other sources. For income above ₹50 lakh or multiple properties, ITR-2 is applicable.

Q5. Is HRA fully exempt from tax?

No, HRA is not fully exempt. The exempt portion is the least of: actual HRA received, 50% of salary (metro) or 40% (non-metro), and actual rent paid minus 10% of salary. The balance is taxable.

Q6. What is the difference between CTC, gross salary, and in-hand salary?

CTC (Cost to Company) is the total annual expenditure by the employer including all benefits. Gross Salary is CTC minus employer contributions to PF and gratuity. In-hand salary is gross salary minus income tax (TDS), professional tax, and employee PF contribution.

Q7. Are perquisites taxable?

Yes, most perquisites are taxable. Examples include rent-free accommodation, car facility, interest-free loans above ₹20,000, and ESOPs. Some perquisites like medical reimbursement up to ₹15,000 and laptop provided for official use are tax-free.

Q8. What is Section 17 of the Income Tax Act?

Section 17 defines 'salary' for income tax purposes. It includes wages, annuity, pension, gratuity, fees, commissions, perquisites, profits in lieu of salary, advance salary, and leave encashment as part of taxable salary.

Q9. What deductions are available under Section 16?

Section 16 allows three deductions: (1) Standard Deduction of ₹75,000 (new regime) or ₹50,000 (old regime), (2) Professional Tax paid by the employee, and (3) Entertainment Allowance for government employees only.

Q10. Is Dearness Allowance taxable?

Yes, Dearness Allowance (DA) is fully taxable for most employees. However, DA that forms part of retirement benefits calculation is considered 'salary' for HRA and gratuity calculations.

Q11. Can I claim both HRA and home loan interest deductions?

Yes, you can claim both HRA exemption and home loan interest deduction under Section 24(b) simultaneously, provided your rented house and owned house are in different cities, or there are valid reasons for not living in the owned property.

Q12. What is Form 16 and why is it important?

Form 16 is a TDS certificate issued by your employer showing details of salary paid and tax deducted during the financial year. It is essential for filing your ITR and serves as proof of income for loan applications.

Q13. Is leave encashment taxable?

Leave encashment received during service is fully taxable. However, leave encashment received at the time of retirement is exempt up to ₹25 lakh (for non-government employees) under Section 10(10AA).

Q14. Which tax regime is better for salaried employees in FY 2025-26?

The new tax regime is better if your deductions are less than ₹3.75 lakh. The old regime benefits those with significant deductions like 80C (₹1.5L), 80D, HRA, and home loan interest. Compare both before deciding.

Q15. What is the maximum tax-free salary in FY 2025-26?

Under the new tax regime, income up to ₹12 lakh is effectively tax-free due to the rebate under Section 87A (for income up to ₹12 lakh) plus the standard deduction of ₹75,000, making income up to ₹12.75 lakh tax-free for salaried employees.

Conclusion

Income from salary is more than just your monthly take-home. It encompasses a wide range of components — from basic pay and allowances to perquisites and retirement benefits. The good news is that the Indian tax law provides several exemptions and deductions that, when used wisely, can significantly reduce your tax burden.

For FY 2025-26, the new tax regime makes income up to ₹12.75 lakh effectively tax-free for salaried employees — an important milestone for middle-income earners. However, whether the old or new regime works for you depends entirely on your specific salary structure, investments, and deductions.

The most important step is to understand your salary slip, collect the right documents, and file your ITR accurately before 31 July 2025. A small mistake can lead to a tax notice; the right tax planning can save you lakhs.
